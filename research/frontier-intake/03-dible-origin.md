## Wave syntheses (verbatim from the agents)

### Wave 1

The only source proposing a German "Dreibelbis" origin for the Dible surname is an anonymous, undated, uncited compiled paper (familiesbyvelmaparsons.com/pdfs/DIBLE.pdf) that grafts the Dible line onto the well-documented immigrant Johanne Jacob Dreibelbis (1709-1761, Richmond Township, Berks County, PA) via a "Jacob Dible" (d. 1 Sep 1820, wife recorded only as "___ Cell") it presents as his son. Cross-checking this claim against independently compiled but well-footnoted Dreibelbis genealogies (citing the 1966/1990 published Dreibelbis family book, DAR Library records, and gravestone readings), Find a Grave's linked family-member memorials, and original 18th-century Richmond Township (Moselem/St. Peter's Reformed) church-book transcriptions, the immigrant's only documented son named Jacob was born 1754, married Maria Magdalena Merkel in 1777, remained in Richmond Township under the Dreibelbis/Treibelbis spelling at least through 1788 as a baptismal sponsor, and died in 1831 - not matching the Parsons paper's unsourced "Jacob Dible, d.1820, wife Cell" in name, wife, date, or place. No child of the immigrant is documented anywhere as moving to western Pennsylvania or changing surname to Dible. The Dible pedigree only becomes independently checkable starting with John Dible/Catherine Heckman (1805-1866/1805-1846), already tied to a real FamilySearch record and buried at Porter Cemetery in what is now Bethel Township, Armstrong County - a real township (incorporated 1878, resolving an apparent Berks-vs-Armstrong location conflict) adjacent to the family's already-documented Kiskiminetas Township base. Given this, the Dreibelbis-to-Dible link reads as an unsupported "famous immigrant" hook rather than a proven connection. A standard surname dictionary instead treats "Dible" as an English variant of "Dibble" or an altered German "Deibel"/"Deubel" - neither has yet been investigated as an alternate origin for this specific Armstrong/Westmoreland County family. WikiTree and Geni pages for the Dreibelbis line are currently behind bot/WAF walls and could not be directly verified; FamilySearch's Malinda Dible Londen profile (naming a father "John Albert Dibler," b. c.1805, Westmoreland Co.) is a promising lead but is a JS-shell page that could not be read directly and needs a real fetch or index pull to confirm.

### Wave 2

This wave overturns the predecessor's implicit framing that the German-origin hypothesis rests solely on the anonymous Parsons PDF. A freely accessible 1890 published county biography (Biographical and Historical Cyclopedia of Westmoreland County, PA, p.661) - a source that pre-dates the Parsons PDF by over a century and is independently sourced from the family itself - states plainly that "the Dibles are of German extraction and originally the name was Diblebiss," and describes an unnamed great-great-grandfather-generation immigrant who was "one of the very earliest settlers" of Franklin Township, Westmoreland County. Crucially, this same 1890 source, plus independently-built WikiTree and Allshouse-family rootsweb trees, all converge on NOT knowing any specific name for Jacob Dible Sr.'s (1780-1872) father - directly undercutting the Parsons PDF's specific, named "Jacob Dible d.1820, wife ___ Cell" claim, which appears in none of these three independent, partly-older compilations. A full direct fetch of WikiTree's 146-profile Dreibelbis index confirms (far more comprehensively than secondary excerpts) that no Dreibelbis descendant ever moved to Westmoreland/Armstrong County or changed surname to Dible. A full fetch of WikiTree's 60-profile Dible index also surfaces a wholly separate, well-documented English Dible lineage (Surrey/Norfolk/Kent/London, 1580s-1948), giving real substance to the competing 'English variant of Dibble' theory. New FamilySearch-profile fetches (via reader-proxy, bypassing the JS-shell problem) sharpen several dates/places (Catherine Heckman's exact birth in 'Washington Parish, Westmoreland,' Malinda Dible Londen's burial at Spring Church, Armstrong Co) but also surface a real unresolved conflict: the Allshouse tree gives John Dible's (1805-1866) wife as an unsourced 'Mrs. Barbara,' not Catherine Heckman, and WikiTree's own build leaves Zephaniah Dible's father entirely unlinked - meaning the very link connecting the project's documented Dible generations back to Jacob Dible Sr. is itself not solid across independent sources. A concrete, unexplored original-record lead was identified: Westmoreland County's 1771-1918 will books and indexes.

### Wave 3

This wave reframes the entire Dible-origin question. The prior Dreibelbis-of-Berks-County hypothesis now looks closed on its own merits: Johann Jacob Dreibelbis's actual 1761 Berks County will (quoted via Annette Burgert's scholarly Palatine Origins of Some Pennsylvania Pioneers, fetched directly from WikiTree) names all seven of his children by name, none of whom left Berks County - a primary-record disconfirmation to add to the predecessor's descendant-index finding. In its place, a genuinely new and far more promising hypothesis surfaced independently from two different compiled trees: the Allshouse family's TNG database (fetched via Wayback Machine, since the live site is CAPTCHA-walled) and FamilySearch's public Family Tree both name Jacob Dible Sr.'s (1780-1872) father as 'Johan Jacob DeVilbiss,' born 1743 to Casper Conrad DeVilbiss and Anne Cocil, married Anna Maria Teagarden in 1778 in Harrison City, Westmoreland County, PA. DeVilbiss is a well-documented American surname (from Alsatian immigrants who signed the 1731 ship Britannia's oath as 'Deibellbissin'/'Debelbesin' and settled Frederick County, Maryland) whose early spellings - Debelbiss, Deubelbiss, Dewelbiss - are a dramatically closer phonetic match to the 1890 Cyclopedia's 'Diblebiss' than 'Dreibelbis' ever was, and whose Maryland-to-southwestern-Pennsylvania migration path fits Westmoreland County's settlement geography far better than an eastern-Berks-County origin would. This remains a compiled-source hypothesis, not a proven one - the chain has no verified primary church, will, or land record yet, and a genuine conflict exists over Johan Jacob DeVilbiss's death (1804 Maryland vs. 1820 Harrison City, PA). Separately, a direct full-text fetch of the 1890 Cyclopedia and, for the first time, a search of the actual 1783/1786 Westmoreland County tax returns (PA Archives 3rd series, vol.22) produced a genuine primary-source hit: 'Dible, Jacob' taxed in Huntingdon Township in 1783 - the earliest documentary trace of the surname in the county, predating Jacob Dible Sr.'s adulthood - though the same surname is entirely absent from the 1786 return. Finally, the John Dible (1805-1866) wife question got more tangled, not less: the Allshouse TNG database's own page for him gives a third, self-flagged-uncertain wife ('Elizabeth Allshouse??', 1806-1856) that matches neither Catherine Heckman nor the predecessor's 'Mrs. Barbara,' and whose children list does not include Zephaniah or John Heckman Dible at all.</summary>
