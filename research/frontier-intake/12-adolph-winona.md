## Where this sits in the tree

Evelyn was raised in Winona, Logan County, KS by 'grandmother Frances Adolph' -- Frances (Rust) Mundell's second marriage explains the name. Waves 1-2 opened the Adolph line (German Baptist Brethren/Dunker origin, Franklin County, KS; Jacob Henry Adolph b. 1857 IN; the 1935 obit placing 'Lon Adolph of Colby'), and wave 3 verified the identification against Find a Grave directly. All of it feeds the case.05 identity question.

## Wave syntheses (verbatim from the agents)

### Wave 1

Built on the already-known baseline (Evelyn Mundell Zimmerman's obituary: raised by grandmother Frances Adolph, graduated Winona High School; and the genealogy-forum lead that Frances Rust divorced Walter Mundell, married a man surnamed Adolph, then after his death married Lyle Strawn and lived in Beloit), this pass identified "Mr. Adolph" as Albert Alonzo "Lon" Adolph (1881-1954) via Find a Grave family-tree cross-links from Mary Frances Rust Strawn's own memorial, and pushed the trail further with several new primary/index hits:

(1) Albert Adolph's family were not part of the Volga-German community associated with Winona -- his father Jacob Henry Adolph's 1935 obituary explicitly ties the family to the "German Baptist Church" (Brethren/Dunker tradition) west of Centropolis, Franklin County, Kansas, with Jacob himself Indiana-born in 1857, arriving in Kansas by his 1875 marriage.

(2) A major primary-source find: the actual 1 April 1950 federal census image for Kingery Township, Thomas County, Kansas (ED 97-9) was pulled directly from NARA's open 1950-census tool (no login required) and shows Evelyn Mundell, age 15, born Colorado, as "granddaughter" in Albert L. and Mary F. Adolph's household -- alongside John D. Adolph (21) and a previously undocumented 13-year-old grandson recorded as "Herbert D.," also Colorado-born. This places the Adolph household in Thomas County rather than Logan County as of census day, an open tension with the "Winona High School" claim worth resolving.

(3) Find a Grave's linked family trees show a likely correction to the existing source ledger: Rex Adolph (1915-1930) appears to be Albert Adolph's biological son, not Walter Mundell's, contra the FamilySearch-derived note currently in the ledger; and only Erma Fay (not Homer Clair) is linked as Walter+Frances's child on Find a Grave.

(4) The "Winona Leader" hypothesis in the task brief appears to be a misnomer: KSGenWeb's 1912 Cyclopedia extract shows Winona's own paper was the "Logan County News" (1904-1943), while "the Leader" was a Russell Springs (county-seat) paper 12 miles away.

(5) The Beloit/Strawn trail is independently corroborated: Frances (as Mary Frances Rust Strawn) died in Hoxie, Sheridan County in 1984 but is buried in Beloit's Elmwood Cemetery; a 1950 census hit shows Edgar Lyle Strawn still married to first wife Dorothy at that date, bounding the Frances-Strawn marriage to after 1954 (Albert's death) and after Dorothy's exit from that marriage. Five research frames with jurisdiction stacks, record sets, and prioritized next pulls are provided below to carry this forward -- covering the Adolph/German-community question, the 1940/1950 census placement conflict, Logan County institutional records, the Strawn/Beloit timeline, and a newly surfaced Rust sibling-count discrepancy.

### Wave 2

Working from the wave-1 baseline (Frances Rust's Mundell-to-Adolph-to-Strawn husband sequence and Albert Alonzo "Lon" Adolph's identification), this pass verified the predecessor's Find a Grave cross-links by fetching the underlying memorials directly and then pushed two more generations deep on both the Rust and Adolph lines, while correcting two of the predecessor's dated/undersourced claims. Key upgrades: (1) Mary Frances Rust Strawn's own Find a Grave family tree now directly names her parents as F. John Rust (1870-1909) and Etta B. Rupert (1876-1952, later Rust Wells) -- resolving the predecessor's tentative Rust/Rupert candidate into a cross-linked identification -- and pushes one generation further to F. John's own parents, John Aissen Rust (1842-1916) and first wife Frauke Folkerts Everwyn Rust (1843-1885), the latter buried unexpectedly in Pekin, Tazewell County, Illinois, opening an East-Frisian-via-Illinois research frontier. (2) On the Adolph side, Jacob Henry Adolph's own Find a Grave record (with his full 1935 obituary text) names his parents as Charles Adolph (1825-1913) and Emerance/"Emmeline" Deubler Adolph (1823-1906); Charles's own memorial carries a documented biography stating he emigrated from Alsace, France in 1843, was son of Peter and Elizabeth (Ruch) Adolph, and married Emerence Deubler in 1847 at New Lisbon, Indiana -- while Emerance's own record gives her birthplace as Stuttgart, Germany, showing two distinct immigrant origins converging in Indiana rather than one homogenous German line. The same obituary independently places Albert "Lon" Adolph in Colby, Thomas County as early as 1935, sharpening the Winona-vs-Thomas-County residency tension. (3) The predecessor's Strawn/Beloit chronology is corrected: Edgar Lyle Strawn's first wife, Dorothy Ellen Pelham Strawn, lived until 1960 and is buried under the Strawn name in the same Beloit cemetery as Frances, meaning the Frances/Strawn marriage window opens no earlier than 1960, not merely "after Albert's 1954 death." (4) The "Winona Leader" newspaper question is further clarified: the Library of Congress confirms the Winona-based Logan County News ran only 1904-1943 -- too early to cover Evelyn's early-1950s graduation -- and the actual continuously-published paper for that era is the county-seat Oakley Graphic (1889-2015+, digitized at the Oakley Public Library), though its search interface proved too JavaScript-heavy for automated querying in this pass. Five sharpened frames with jurisdiction stacks, record sets, and prioritized next pulls are provided below, and the possibly-living "Herbert D." lead from wave 1 was deliberately left unresearched further per the living-persons safeguard.

### Wave 3

This pass verified the wave-1/2 Adolph identification by fetching Find A Grave memorials directly (not secondhand) and cross-checking a rich, previously untapped compiled genealogy (Carrie Hall's rootsweb pages transcribing an academic coverlet-weaving history, an 1843 ship manifest, Wayne County IN naturalization/marriage records, and three original newspaper obituaries from 1907, 1914, and 1922-23). Three major upgrades: (1) RESOLVED the predecessor's open question -- Henry Adolph, the Civil War-era Dade County MO/Douglas County KS Brethren coverlet weaver, is verified via his own 1907 obituary as the full brother of Charles Adolph and George Adolph. (2) NEW GENERATION on the Deubler side -- Emerance Deubler Adolph's own Find A Grave memorial names her parents as Konstantin Deubler and Franciska Noll Deubler of Stuttgart, extending the German line one generation past what wave 2 reported, though the trail now ends there absent an original Stuttgart church-register hit. (3) MAJOR CHRONOLOGY CORRECTION -- a 1922 Colby newspaper clipping (embedded verbatim on a child's Find A Grave memorial) places 'Mr. and Mrs. Lon Adolph' already living in Colby, Thomas County by August 1922 after relocating from the Adolph family's ancestral Scranton, Osage County home, and Find A Grave's reciprocal family-tree data (confirmed on Frances's own memorial) attributes five children born 1915-1929 to Albert Adolph AND Mary Frances Rust -- meaning the Rust-to-Adolph transition happened around 1913-1915, roughly four decades earlier than the vague 'later in life' framing implied by prior waves, and squarely centered on Thomas County, not Logan County. A live Find A Grave surname search of Winona Cemetery returned zero hits for Adolph, Mundell, Rust, or Strawn, meaning the Winona/Logan County connection currently rests entirely on Evelyn's own obituary wording with no independent primary corroboration found this pass -- a sharp, well-defined frame for the next researcher rather than a settled fact. The Alsace-commune mystery behind 'Elmo, Alsace, France' remains unresolved at the European edge of the record trail; it now appears twice independently (Charles's Find A Grave bio and Henry's 1907 obituary transcription), ruling out a one-off transcription slip but still lacking any match to an actual Bas-Rhin/Haut-Rhin commune, so the line terminates there pending a Bas-Rhin/Haut-Rhin parish-register search.
