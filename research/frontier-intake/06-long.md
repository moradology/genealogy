## Wave syntheses (verbatim from the agents)

### Wave 1

The 1880 Chapman 'History of Pike County, Illinois' bio of John F. Long is now confirmed word-for-word via the archive.org OCR text: he was 'born in Tennessee in 1805, son of Robert and Betsy (Wasson) Long, natives of North Carolina.' The same primary source gives John F. Long's marriage (23 Feb 1826, Vanderburgh Co, IN, to Sally A. Patton), 8 children (7 living in 1880: Phoebe J., Robert N., William H., James H., Samuel A., Mary E., Sarah M.), his 1828 arrival west of Milton, Pike Co, and his section-32 farm. A second, much richer source -- the Find a Grave memorial for Rev. Robert Long (68978483), written by a self-identified descendant citing an Iredell Co, NC deed abstract, the 1850/1790 censuses, and a Bone-family history -- lays out a full (but compiled, not court-verified) narrative: Robert b. 27 Apr 1775 (Watauga settlement/4th Creek Statesville area), in father Samuel Long's household in the 1790 Iredell Co, NC census; sold land there ~1799-1800 (witnessed a 1807 Wasson deed in Iredell Co); married Betsy Wasson c.1800; moved to Williamson/Maury Co, TN by 1805 (where John Franklin Long and Abner Nelson Long were born); moved to Cynthiana, Posey Co, IN in 1810 with McCreary/Wasson kin, built the first grist mill there in 1811; Betsy died 1818-1822 in Cynthiana; Robert remarried Sarah H. Blackburn Davis in 1823; the family moved to McDonough Co, IL (1830s) then Van Buren/Jefferson Co, IA (1835-36), where Robert died 12 Dec 1850 and is buried at Smith Cemetery, Locust Grove Twp. The same bio floats an Ulster-Scot origin hypothesis (Chester Co PA -> Great Valley Road -> Chestnut Level, Lancaster Co PA -> Augusta Co VA -> NC) that is speculative/uncited to a primary record, and separately claims 'Robert Long's line did marry into the German Long line in Pike Co, IL.' I could not independently confirm that specific intermarriage in the primary Chapman text: the two Chapman-documented Long marriages I found (Phoebe [daughter of Abner Nelson] Long m. David Foreman, 1859; Emily [daughter of John Franklin?] Long m. Harrison C. French, 1865) both look like they involve Robert Long's own descendants rather than a distinct German-immigrant Long family. Chapman's book does independently document a separate, seemingly unrelated Long family in Pearl Township (Thomas S. Long, b. 1807 Bucks Co, PA, son of Morgan and Rachel [Deemer] Long, who moved to Illinois in 1835) that is a plausible candidate for the 'German Long' line the bio references, but no marriage link between the two Long lines was found in the text pulled. The 1879 Western Historical Co. 'History of Jefferson County, Iowa' (vol. historyofjeffers01west) does NOT mention 'Long' by that surname anywhere in its full OCR text, so it is not the source of the Robert Long church/biographical narrative in the Find a Grave bio (that bio actually cites a 1850 census and an external genealogy.com page, not a Jefferson Co county history) -- a companion/second Jefferson Co history (Fulton, 1912-14, 2 vols) has not yet been checked and should be.

### Wave 2

This wave verified several of the wave-1 predecessor's strongest unverified leads against independent primary sources and pushed the frontier back one to two generations, while also correcting some framing.

Biggest wins: (1) Fulton's 1912-14 History of Jefferson County, Iowa (vol. 1, p.220), previously unchecked, independently confirms Rev. Robert Long organized the first Christian church at Abingdon (dated 1843, not 1844 as the compiled bio says), and a transcribed 1844 Polk Township election poll list independently places both 'Robert Long' and 'William K. Long' there as voters, plus Robert Long and stepson James B. Davis as 1845 election judges -- real county-history corroboration the wave-1 report said was still needed. (2) Goodspeed's 1886 History of Posey County, Indiana, not previously checked, independently corroborates the Cynthiana/Posey Co IN material in the compiled Find a Grave bio almost verbatim: Robert Long supplied logs for the town's first church and built its first mill, and Rev. Joseph Wasson's 1816 splinter congregation at 'Liberty' (the 'Wasonites') is documented exactly as claimed. (3) Chalkley's published Augusta County, VA court-record abstracts independently verify, near word-for-word, the deepest-generation deed citations (Samuel Long to John Long/Henry Long/James Young, Buffalo Creek, 1764) in the Find a Grave bio for the candidate father Samuel Long, and a WikiTree transcription of the actual 1790 Iredell Co NC census confirms the household composition (2 free males 16+, 2 under 16, 2 females, no slaves) the bio describes for that same Samuel Long -- though the bio's own author explicitly hedges this Samuel-to-Robert link as only 'possible,' not proven, which corrects the wave-1 report's more confident framing of it.

Two open questions from wave 1 were sharpened rather than closed. The 'Emily, daughter of John Long' question: Chapman's actual 1880 text (verified) does say 'daughter of John Long,' and the compiler's claim that this is really Sarah Emily Long (daughter of Abner Nelson Long, independently documented marrying the same Harrison C. French in 1865) is internally consistent but remains an uncited correction. The 1823 marriage-location question was previously framed as Posey Co IN vs. Lawrence Co IL; closer reading shows the real, still-unresolved contradiction is between the Robert Long memorial (1823, Lawrence Co IL, Rev. Kinkade) and the Betsy Wasson memorial (c.1822, Harrison Co KY) -- both written by the same compiler.

The 'German Long intermarriage in Pike Co IL' claim was tested hardest and came up unsupported: a full read of the entire (duplicated) Find a Grave bio text found no specific marriage record anywhere backing it, two of the bio's own named 'German Long' exemplars (Nicholas and Simeon Long) are actually buried in Vanderburgh Co IN rather than Pike Co IL, and the one clearly German-associated Long family Chapman's Pike Co history does document (Thomas S. Long/Morgan Long/Rachel Deemer, Pearl Township) shows no marriage connection to Robert Long's line in the text. A genuinely German-immigrant Rowan Co NC 'Long' line (Johannes Long > Jacob Long m. Elizabeth Klinard/Clinard) was newly identified as the likely origin of the 'German Long' label, but its Pike Co IL trail is not yet built.

### Wave 3

This wave pushed the Long line back to its documented colonial floor and tightened several of the predecessor's open questions with primary and near-primary text I read directly rather than via secondary paraphrase. Biggest confirmation: I fetched the actual archive.org full text of Lyman Chalkley's "Chronicles of the Scotch-Irish Settlement in Virginia" (Vol. 2) and located the real page-210 abstract of Joseph Long's 11 Sep 1757 Augusta Co, VA will, which does name a son Samuel -- resolving, at verified-primary confidence, the predecessor's open question about whether that will names Samuel at all (it does; whether that Samuel is Robert Long's father remains the separate, still-open question). I also fetched WikiTree's compiled Joseph Long profile directly, which adds a documented 1736 Lancaster County, PA court appearance for Joseph Long alongside Samuel Crocket -- placing the Long-Crockett family in the Chestnut Level Ulster-Scots Presbyterian settlement area before their 1740s move to Augusta Co, VA, the classic Great Wagon Road migration pattern -- though Joseph's own birthplace (WikiTree hedges "Ireland," the Find a Grave bio floats an unsourced "Londonderry, Ireland or Lancaster Co, PA") remains unproven by any ship, naturalization, or parish record. A full direct read of Rev. Robert Long's own Find a Grave bio (rather than relying on the predecessor's summary of it) turned up several previously-unflagged, citable details: a published Iredell Co NC deed abstract (Russell C. Black, Books E&F, 1803-1808) naming Robert Long as a legal witness in 1807/1808, anchoring him at Fourth Creek right up to that date; a specific claim that the actual 1850 census and a death-record index both give Robert's birthplace as Tennessee (contradicting the 1880 county history's North Carolina nativity claim); and the bio's own explicit, non-conflated listing of three alternate "possible relative" candidates. Critically, I traced and corrected a misattribution in the predecessor's report: the "Betsy Wasson memorial" they cited as contradicting Robert's own marriage-date claim does not exist (Betsy has no Find a Grave memorial or known burial site at all) -- the real second, contradicting source is Sarah H. Blackburn Long's own memorial, which quotes a named published book ("Blackburn's Today and Yesterday" by Frances Hilliard Blackburn, p.237) giving "probably 1822" with no location, against Robert's own memorial's confident "1823, Lawrence Co, IL, Rev. William Kinkade." Finally, for the Y-DNA open question, I found and directly read the Long Surname DNA Project's progenitors page, which documents confirmed Y-DNA testers descending from two of Joseph Long's other sons (Henry, 1730-1809, and John, 1735-1826) grouped in "Subgroup P" with haplogroup R-L21 (DF21+, Z246+, DF25+, DF5+) -- a concrete, actionable comparison target for a future Robert Long male-line descendant, though no such test has been run. The trail's hard stop for European origins is Joseph Long's 1736 Lancaster Co, PA record; no passenger list, naturalization record, or Ulster parish register has been located for him, and the next records that could extend it (Chestnut Level/Middle Octorara PA congregational records, PRONI's free Ulster catalogue, or a Y-DNA match) are named in the frames below.
