## Where this sits in the tree

The Zimmerman chain: Norfolk, NE (1880 household, documented) -> Cawker City/Beloit, Mitchell County, KS (1895 marriage, strong) -> Selden, Sheridan County. Whether this was Catholic or Lutheran chain migration determines which sacramental registers hold Michael's and Leonard's records -- the congregational identification is the practical key for case.02 and case.03 (Leonard-to-Michael hardening, Doyle's missing 2005 obituary).

## Original frame given to the agents

> ZIMMERMAN COMMUNITY: The Zimmerman chain Norfolk NE (1880) to Cawker City/Beloit, Mitchell Co KS (1895+) to Selden, Sheridan Co KS. Was this a German colony chain migration (Catholic or Lutheran)? ## Wave syntheses (verbatim from the agents)

### Wave 1

Norfolk, Madison County NE was founded in 1866 as a German-Lutheran colony from Ixonia, Wisconsin, whose first church was St. Paul's Lutheran - but a direct read of the 1880 census index (not previously extracted in index.html beyond the household roster) shows every Zimmerman household member, parents and children alike, self-reporting birthplace as 'Bavaria,' contradicting the unsourced FamilySearch claim that son Michael was born at 'Wuerttemberger Hof, Mainhardt' (a Lutheran/Evangelical parish by its own church history). By the time the family resurfaces at Cawker City/Beloit, Mitchell County KS (Michael's 1895 marriage to Catholic-heritage Elizabeth Nauer) and later Selden, every documented congregation touchpoint is Catholic and heavily German: Saints Peter and Paul at Cawker City (German-language Masses to 1915), St. John the Baptist at Beloit (about two-thirds German by 1883), Immaculate Conception at Leoville (served Selden's Catholics before 1906), and finally Doyle Zimmerman's 1954 wedding at Sacred Heart, Colby. The Kansas leg of the chain therefore reads unambiguously Catholic; the open puzzle is the Nebraska/German origin stage, where the family's own record conflicts with the compiled tree attribution, and no direct evidence yet fixes which specific parish register (if any) recorded the 1895 marriage or Leonard Zimmerman's own baptism.

### Wave 2

Re-reading the 1880 Madison County NE census PDF at the raw-data level (rather than the household-roster summary already in index.html) produces the strongest new evidence in this pass: parents John and Catherine and their three oldest children (John Jr, Michael, Paul) all report Bavaria as birthplace, but the two youngest (Baptist, b. c.1876, and Louisa, b. 1880) were born in Nebraska - fixing the family's emigration to a c.1873-76 window and showing no Wisconsin-born child at all, unlike at least six literal next-door Norfolk households (Len, Boche, Sidschlag, Pribnow, Machmuller, Bergman) whose census rows show the classic German-to-Wisconsin-to-Nebraska sequence of the 1866-68 Ixonia colonists. This directly answers the predecessor's open question about colony membership: the Zimmermans were not part of the original Wisconsin-Lutheran wagon train: they were Bavarian immigrants who arrived independently a decade later and settled among the established Wisconsin-origin neighbors, which explains co-location without shared origin. Verified, quote-sourced parish histories confirm Norfolk had no Catholic parish until 1881 (served from West Point before that) and that West Point's own St. Mary's parish was only founded in 1875 - meaning any Catholic Zimmerman baptism between 1876-1880 would fall to a very newly organized West Point parish or an even earlier circuit-riding priest, sharpening but not yet closing the custody question. The most significant new lead is a cross-surname discovery: a "Joseph Zimmerman" appears by name among the roughly 30 founding families of Leoville, Decatur County KS in 1885, in the identical short list that includes John Zodrow (the documented ancestor on Doyle Zimmerman's maternal Zodrow line) - raising the possibility that the Zimmerman and Zodrow families were already linked a generation before Leonard Zimmerman married Cecilia Zodrow in Selden. A check of the 1895 Kansas state census for Decatur County found no Zimmerman household there, so this Joseph Zimmerman (if related) had left or died by 1895. The New Almelo-Leoville-Selden Catholic circuit was run by the same Precious Blood missionary (Fr. August Reichert) across all three towns, and New Almelo's St. Joseph parish is already tied to the family through Catherina Koeberger Nauer's 1906 burial there - suggesting these three Sheridan/Decatur/Norton county missions functioned as one circuit whose surviving registers (if any predate individual parish organization) may be held by the Diocese of Salina or the Precious Blood order's own archive rather than by an individual parish. FamilySearch's Michael/Paul Zimmerman "Wuerttemberger Hof, Mainhardt" ancestor-profile pages remain unreachable as JS shells even on direct fetch, so that unsourced claim is neither confirmed nor refuted by this pass, only further undercut by the census's internally consistent Bavaria self-reports. Cached files from a prior research pass show the German Emigrants Database (Deutsche Auswanderer-Datenbank) was already searched for Zimmerman/Zinkl name variants with zero results, so that specific free passenger-list lead is exhausted absent new spelling variants or a Castle Garden cross-check.

### Wave 3

This pass independently re-verified the 1880 census at the byte level (own pdftotext extraction, not a re-read of predecessor's summary) and confirms the Bavaria/Bavaria/Bavaria x5 pattern with no Wisconsin-born child, cementing that the Norfolk NE Zimmermans arrived directly from Bavaria c.1873-76 rather than through the Wisconsin-Lutheran Ixonia colony chain. The bigger structural finding is that the 'colony chain' question has two different correct answers at two different links in the chain: NO for the 1880 Norfolk household (independent Bavarian Catholic arrival, not colony transit), but quite possibly YES one generation and one state later - Wikipedia states in so many words that 'Leoville was founded in 1885 by a colony of German Catholics,' and the verbatim founder roster names both Joseph Zimmerman and John Zodrow in the same list, six years before Leonard Zimmerman would marry Cecilia Zodrow in Selden. Every religious marker across the whole family (Cawker City Catholic marriage 1895, shared Selden Catholic-cemetery stone, Waterloo-Ontario-Catholic Nauer in-laws, New Almelo St Joseph burial, the Precious Blood Reichert circuit) is Catholic; there is no Lutheran thread anywhere in this line. The diocesan/parish custody stack is now fully mapped on both ends with fresh primary-page quotes I pulled directly (not relayed from the predecessor): on the Nebraska side, Norfolk's own Sacred Heart parish only dates to 1881, so the two Nebraska-born children (Baptist c.1876, Louisa 1880) would fall to either Battle Creek's St Patrick's (organized 1874, in use from May 1875) or West Point's St Mary's/Assumption (founded 1875 by Fr. Uhing), both now under the Archdiocese of Omaha Chancery Archives; on the Kansas side, Cawker City's own Sts Peter & Paul parish was already organized in 1878 (Mass there as early as 1873), seventeen years before the 1895 marriage, making it - not Beloit - the primary register target, under the Diocese of Leavenworth-to-Concordia(1887)-to-Salina(1944) chain, with the New Almelo/Leoville/Selden mission circuit run by Precious Blood Fr. August Reichert from 1878-1889 across 'the northern two tiers of counties, from Beloit to the Colorado line.' A genuinely new negative-finding closes off a dead-end cleanly: the Kansas Historical Society's own archives-holdings page for Mitchell County lists only Glen Elder township items (council minutes, JP dockets, an 1895 census roll) and no marriage records, and FamilySearch's Kansas County Marriages index likewise does not cover Mitchell County - so the 19 Nov 1895 civil marriage license/return has never been digitized and sits only in the Mitchell County Clerk's office in Beloit. The trail on the European side currently ends at 'Bavaria' with no named town: the FamilySearch 'Wuerttemberger Hof, Mainhardt' claim (an Evangelical Lutheran parish in a different German state entirely) looks like a wrong-tree misattribution given the internally consistent, five-times-repeated Bavaria self-report, and further Archion/Mainhardt spend is not recommended. The next record that would actually extend the line past Bavaria is not a European one yet - it is an American baptismal or marriage register (Battle Creek, West Point, or Cawker City) that might name a specific Bavarian Heimat parish, which is standard practice in period Catholic sacramental entries; absent that, the correct European target becomes a Bavarian Catholic emigration finding aid (Auswandererkartei/Landesarchiv Bayern), not the Wuerttemberg Evangelical church books currently queued.
