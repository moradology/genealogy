## Where this sits in the tree

Catherine M. Zinkle (b. 1839, 'Bavaria' per the 1880 census) married John Paul Zimmerman in 1864 'in Bavaria' per the FamilySearch index; they are Doyle's great-grandparents (the 1880 Norfolk household is a documented event). Case.02 hinges on whether Zinkle/Zimmermann were really at Wuerttemberger Hof/Mainhardt: the free Mainhardt village database returns ZERO for both names (a load-bearing negative consistent with 'Bavaria'), and wave 3 found that database is scriptable (personen.genealogie-mainhardt.de). A Catholic Bavarian Zinkle cluster exists at Prairie du Chien, WI.

## Original frame given to the agents

> ZINKLE: Catherine M. Zinkle/Zinkl (1839 Bavaria/Germany - m. John Paul Zimmerman 1864 'in Bavaria' per FS index), of Wuerttemberger Hof/Mainhardt. Zinkle is an odd form - frame variants (Zinkel, Zünkel, Zinckel), Mainhardt-area Evangelical registers, Wuerttemberg emigration indexes (Auswanderung), and the Bavaria-vs-Wuerttemberg contradiction.

## Wave syntheses (verbatim from the agents)

### Wave 1

Frontier: Catherine M. Zinkle/Zinkl (b.1839, Bavaria/Germany per FamilySearch) m. John Paul Zimmerman (b.1842, Bavaria/Germany) in 1864 'in Bavaria' per FS index; the couple's children Michael (b.1869) and Paul (b.1872) are instead indexed at Wuerttemberger Hof, Mainhardt (Baden-Wuerttemberg) - the Bavaria-vs-Wuerttemberg contradiction this pass targeted. Key new findings: (1) Wuerttemberger Hof is a real, tiny (38 residents in 1852) Wuerttemberg hamlet of Huetten/Mainhardt, confirmed via LEO-BW and German Wikipedia, transferred from the Palatinate to Wuerttemberg in 1504; Huetten was ecclesiastically a filial of Mainhardt's Evangelical parish since 1486 (validating the project's existing Archion Mainhardt targets) but was under a DIFFERENT civil Oberamt (Gaildorf, 1808-1938) than Mainhardt village proper (Oberamt Weinsberg) during the relevant 1864-1872 window - a previously undocumented distinction that changes which Wuerttemberg Emigration Index volume (Vol.5/Gaildorf vs Vol.6/Weinsberg) and which LEO-BW 'Amt' search term to use. (2) I directly queried the free Genealogie Mainhardt village database for every plausible Zink* variant (Zinkel, Zinckel, Zuenkel, Zinkl, Zink, Zinck, Zenkel, Zinckler) plus Zimmerman/Zimmermann; all returned zero results except an unrelated 16th-century Zinck family at Baeumlesfeld - real negative evidence, though the crowdsourced database is admittedly incomplete. (3) LEO-BW's free Auswanderer aus Suedwestdeutschland database (~260,000 records) confirmed indexing at least one Wuerttemberger Hof emigrant (unrelated, 1804) and genuinely processed (not merely failed) a zero-result search for 'Zimmerman', but its JS/Liferay architecture defeated attempts to script a full Zinkel/Zimmerman query - flagged as an interactive next_pull. (4) The Wuerttemberg Emigration Index book is login/loan-gated on Internet Archive and paywalled on Ancestry - no free full-text access found. (5) No WikiTree profile and no locatable Find A Grave memorial exist yet for John Paul Zimmerman or a matching Catherine Zinkle in Kansas; a same-surname, Bavaria-connected 'Zinkle' family was found in Prairie du Chien, Wisconsin, but is unproven as related. (6) The FamilySearch Ancestors profile pages remain unreadable JS shells without login, so the record source behind the 'Bavaria, 1864' tag could not be identified - this is the single highest-value next pull. Four actionable frames follow below.

### Wave 2

Wave 2 pushed on three fronts: (1) re-tested every automated path into FamilySearch (Ancestors profile, record-search results, and catalog pages) and confirmed the JS-shell block is site-wide, not just the Ancestors page the predecessor flagged, and that no Wayback Machine snapshot or search-engine cache exists to substitute for it. (2) Found and queried a previously untried, genuinely free, scriptable search tool -- the Deutsche Auswanderer-Datenbank (Bremen-port emigrant index) at dad-recherche.de -- which returned real hits for 'Zinkel' (33 records, 1859-1891) and 'Zinkl' (1 record, ZINKL FRANZ, 1871) but zero for 'Zinckel'/'Zuenkel' and zero for any 'Zimmermann' in the 1872-1880 window the family likely emigrated in, the last being a probable coverage gap rather than a true negative. (3) Used modern surname-geography data (forebears.io) to show 'Zinkl' is 96% Bavaria-concentrated within Germany versus only 1% in Baden-Wuerttemberg, and that the entire US 'Zinkle' surname population is a tiny (64-person) cluster 61% concentrated in Wisconsin -- almost certainly the same Prairie du Chien/Crawford County, Bavaria-born Zinkle family the predecessor had flagged as an unproven same-surname coincidence. Pulled the actual Find a Grave bios for that Wisconsin couple (Thomas Zinkle, b. 3 Apr 1864 Bavaria; wife Catherine 'Kate' Gillitzer, b. 21 Dec 1864 Bavaria, married at St. Gabriel's Catholic church 1890) and flag a new tension: that family is Catholic, while the project's working Mainhardt hypothesis for the target family is Evangelical, which argues against treating the Wisconsin cluster as a proxy for Catherine M. Zinkle's origin even though it is statistically almost the only 'Zinkle' population in America. Also resolved (in the predecessor's favor) a bibliographic conflict about the Wuerttemberg Emigration Index: an Allen County Public Library research-guide PDF claims the 8 volumes are alphabetical by surname (which would misdirect research to Volume 7), but a more detailed, internally-consistent source (germanroots.com, with a verifiable worked example) confirms the index is organized by Oberamt of departure and that Volume 5 covers Gaildorf (Wuerttemberger Hof/Huetten's Oberamt) while Volume 6 covers Weinsberg (Mainhardt village's Oberamt) -- exactly the split the predecessor had derived independently, now corroborated by an outside source. Could not push a further generation back on either parent line; every direct-record avenue (Archion image content, GEDBAS, FamilySearch record images, HathiTrust full-text, Matricula Online name search) remains genuinely gated behind login, paywall, or bot-protection that resisted repeated automated retries, which is reported honestly rather than guessed around.

### Wave 3

This wave's highest-value new find is a genuinely free, no-login, scriptable local database - Genealogie Mainhardt (personen.genealogie-mainhardt.de, ~30,279 persons, c.1450-present) - that was queried across every plausible Zink*/Zimmermann spelling variant and returned zero hits for 'Zimmermann' anywhere and zero hits for six Zink* variants, with the single exception of an unrelated 'Zinck' family (Bäumlesfeld, 1552-1577) that married out of the surname within one generation. Because the same database independently holds 205 birth records at the exact hamlet 'Württemberger Hof' (population only 38 in 1852) spanning centuries through 1890, this is substantive negative evidence that either the family was not long-rooted in Mainhardt, or FamilySearch's 'Wuerttemberger Hof' locality tag for Michael John Zimmerman Sr. and Paul Michael Zimmerman is an indexer inference rather than a confirmed church-book fact. Also found, via Wikipedia, that Württemberger Hof/Bäumlesfeld came to Württemberg already in 1504 (via Amt Weinsberg from the Palatinate) - three centuries before the surrounding Mainhardt/Hütten territory left Hohenlohe rule in 1806 - a jurisdictional nuance relevant to which pre-1806 archive would hold the earliest records. On the emigration-record front, this wave corrected a prior-wave claim: using the actual live Deutsche Auswanderer-Datenbank search tool (dad-recherche.de/hmb/search.asp, not the bare redirect domain), 'Zimmermann' is an extremely common surname with 656 Bremen-emigrant hits in 1872-1880 alone (capped at 100 shown online), and narrowing to 1874 specifically surfaced an age-consistent, unconfirmed candidate pair - 'Johann Zimmermann' age 31 and 'Catherina Zimmermann' age 34 - whose ages line up almost exactly with John Paul Zimmerman (b. 1842) and Catherine M. Zinkle (b. 1839) if they emigrated in early 1874 before that year's birthdays. This is circumstantial, not proof, and the free tier gives no birthplace or family linkage; the paid DAD Auftrags-Recherche or an in-person Bremerhaven museum visit is the concrete next record. Also confirmed (with a different tool than before) that FamilySearch Ancestors pages remain a pure JS shell to any automated fetch; that genealogy.net's GOV/GenWiki gazetteer is blocked by an explicit anti-scraper (not paywall) 'Anubis' challenge solvable by an ordinary human browser; that Geneanet's search is likewise JS-rendered and automation-blocked despite showing 9 raw index hits for 'ZINKL Catharina' in its embedded metadata; and that the Internet Archive's copy of the Wuerttemberg Emigration Index is a real, immediately-borrowable (zero-waitlist) free book whose text is nonetheless walled off from every unauthenticated automated request (401/403), meaning a human with a free IA login - not a script - is the correct next actor. A compiled surname-history source also points a slightly sharper finger at the Oberpfalz (Regensburg/Parsberg area) rather than Franconia as the Bavarian region worth prioritizing, via two centuries-old namesakes, though this remains directional only. No new primary-record breakthrough was achieved on either parent generation; the frontier remains exactly where the predecessor left it, now with sharper jurisdiction detail, a corrected emigration-database read, and one concrete, named, payable next record to chase.
